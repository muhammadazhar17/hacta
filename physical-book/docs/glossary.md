# Glossary of Terms: Physical AI & Humanoid Robotics

## A

**Actuator**: A component of a robot that converts control signals into physical motion. Examples include servo motors, stepper motors, and hydraulic cylinders.

**Anthropomorphic**: Having human-like characteristics, particularly in the context of robot design where robots have a human-like form and structure.

**Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)**: A type of artificial intelligence with the ability to understand, learn, and apply knowledge across a wide range of tasks at a human-like level of competence.

**Autonomous System**: A system that operates independently without direct human control, making decisions based on its programming and sensor inputs.

## B

**Behavior Tree**: A hierarchical structure used in robotics and artificial intelligence to organize and execute complex behaviors in a modular and reusable way.

**Biomechanics**: The study of the structure, function, and motion of the mechanical aspects of biological systems, particularly the human body.

**Bounding Box**: A rectangular box used in computer graphics and robotics to enclose an object for collision detection and spatial reasoning.

## C

**Cartesian Space**: The three-dimensional space defined by X, Y, and Z coordinates used to describe positions in the physical world.

**Center of Mass (COM)**: The point in a body where all the mass can be considered to be concentrated for the purpose of analyzing motion and balance.

**Collision Detection**: The computational problem of detecting the intersection of two or more objects in space, used extensively in robotics and simulation.

**Compliance Control**: A control strategy that allows a robot to adapt to environmental forces by adjusting its position or force output.

**Computer Vision**: A field of artificial intelligence that trains computers to interpret and understand the visual world through digital images and videos.

**Control Loop**: A feedback mechanism in which a system continuously monitors its output and adjusts its behavior to maintain a desired state.

**Cortical Column**: A functional unit of the mammalian cerebral cortex, consisting of layers of neurons interconnected in a stereotypical pattern.

## D

**Deep Learning**: A subset of machine learning that uses multi-layered neural networks to model and understand complex patterns in data.

**Degrees of Freedom (DOF)**: The number of independent parameters that define the configuration or state of a mechanical system, such as a robot joint.

**Distributed Intelligence**: A system architecture where intelligence is spread across multiple agents or components rather than centralized in a single location.

**Dynamics**: The branch of mechanics concerned with the motion of bodies under the action of forces.

## E

**Embodied AI**: Artificial intelligence that is integrated into physical systems, enabling interaction with the real world through sensors and actuators.

**End Effector**: The device at the end of a robot arm designed to interact with the environment, such as a gripper or tool.

**Episodic Memory**: The memory system that enables the recollection of specific events and experiences from an individual's past.

**Exoskeleton**: An external framework worn by a person to enhance strength, endurance, or mobility.

## F

**Forward Kinematics**: The use of joint parameters to compute the configuration of the end-effector of a robot arm.

**Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM)**: A 3D printing process that uses a continuous filament of thermoplastic material to create objects.

## G

**Gazebo**: A robotics simulation environment that provides realistic physics simulation and rendering capabilities.

**Generalized Coordinates**: A set of parameters that define the configuration of a mechanical system relative to a reference configuration.

**Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)**: A class of machine learning frameworks designed by the competition between two neural networks.

**Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS)**: A satellite-based system that provides geolocation and time information anywhere on or near the Earth.

## H

**Haptic Feedback**: Technology that simulates the sense of touch by applying forces, vibrations, or motions to the user.

**Hebbian Learning**: A neuroscientific theory based on the principle that neurons that fire together wire together.

**Heterogeneous Computing**: An architecture that uses two or more different types of processors that work together to execute instructions.

**Human-Robot Interaction (HRI)**: The study of interactions between humans and robots, including social, cognitive, and physical aspects.

## I

**Inverse Kinematics**: The mathematical process of determining the joint parameters needed to place the end-effector of a robot arm in a desired position.

**Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU)**: An electronic device that measures and reports a body's specific force, angular rate, and sometimes the magnetic field surrounding the body.

**Isaac Sim**: NVIDIA's robotics simulation application based on the Omniverse platform for developing and testing AI-based robotics applications.

## J

**Joint Space**: The space defined by the joint angles of a robot, as opposed to Cartesian space which describes position in 3D space.

**Jacobian Matrix**: A matrix of partial derivatives that describes the relationship between velocities in Cartesian space and joint space for a robot.

## K

**Kinematics**: The study of motion without considering the forces that cause the motion.

**Kinesthetic Intelligence**: The ability to use one's body effectively to solve problems or create products, involving mental imagery and physical awareness.

## L

**LIDAR (Light Detection and Ranging)**: A remote sensing method that uses light in the form of a pulsed laser to measure distances.

**Locomotion**: The ability to move from one place to another, particularly relevant in mobile robotics and humanoid walking.

**Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)**: A type of recurrent neural network that can learn and remember long-term dependencies.

## M

**Machine Learning**: A type of artificial intelligence that enables computers to learn and make decisions from data without being explicitly programmed.

**Manipulation**: The ability of a robot to reposition objects in its environment using effectors such as robotic hands.

**Middleware**: Software that provides common services and capabilities to applications beyond what's provided by the operating system.

**Mobile Robot**: A robot that can move through its environment, as opposed to fixed robots like industrial arms.

**Motion Planning**: The computational problem of automatically determining a path for a robot to navigate from a starting point to a destination while avoiding obstacles.

## N

**Navigation2 (Nav2)**: The navigation stack for ROS 2, providing path planning and execution capabilities for mobile robots.

**Neuromorphic Computing**: A computing paradigm that mimics the neural structure and function of the human brain.

**Non-Holonomic System**: A mechanical system with constraints that cannot be integrated to form holonomic constraints.

## O

**Omnidirectional**: The ability to move in any direction, often used to describe robots that can move in any direction on a plane.

**Omniverse**: NVIDIA's platform for 3D simulation and design collaboration that supports Isaac Sim for robotics.

**OpenCV**: An open-source computer vision and machine learning software library.

**Operational Space Control**: A control framework that allows robots to be controlled directly in Cartesian space rather than joint space.

## P

**Parallel Computing**: A type of computation in which many calculations or processes are carried out simultaneously.

**Path Planning**: The computational process of determining the optimal route for a robot to navigate from its current location to a destination.

**Physical AI**: The field that combines artificial intelligence with physical systems, enabling intelligent agents to interact with the physical world.

**Point Cloud**: A set of data points in space, commonly produced by 3D scanning devices and used in robotics and computer vision.

**Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) Controller**: A control loop mechanism employing feedback that is widely used in industrial control systems.

## Q

**Quality of Service (QoS)**: In ROS 2, a set of policies that determine how messages are delivered across a network, including reliability, durability, and liveliness.

**Quaternion**: A mathematical concept used to represent rotations in 3D space, commonly used in robotics for attitude representation.

## R

**Recurrent Neural Network (RNN)**: A class of artificial neural networks where connections between nodes form a directed graph along a temporal sequence.

**Redundant Robot**: A robot with more degrees of freedom than necessary to perform a given task, providing flexibility in motion planning.

**Robot Operating System (ROS/ROS 2)**: Flexible frameworks for writing robot software, providing hardware abstraction, device drivers, and libraries.

**ROS 2**: The second generation of the Robot Operating System with improved architecture for real-time and safety-critical applications.

**ROS Navigation Stack (Nav2)**: Software packages that provide path planning, obstacle avoidance, and localization for mobile robots.

**ROS Middleware (RMW)**: The abstraction layer in ROS 2 that allows different DDS implementations to work as transport mechanisms.

## S

**Sensor Fusion**: The process of combining sensory data or data derived from disparate sources to achieve better information than could be achieved by means of one source alone.

**Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM)**: The computational problem of constructing or updating a map of an unknown environment while simultaneously keeping track of an agent's location within it.

**Singularity**: In robotics, a configuration where the robot loses one or more degrees of freedom, often causing control problems.

**Social Robot**: A robot designed to interact with humans in a natural, social way using verbal and non-verbal communication.

**Stability Margin**: A measure of how close a system is to becoming unstable, important in robot balance and control.

**State Estimation**: The process of estimating the internal state of a dynamic system from measurements of its inputs and outputs.

## T

**Tensor Processing Unit (TPU)**: An AI accelerator application-specific integrated circuit developed by Google for neural network machine learning.

**Time-Sensitive Networking (TSN)**: A set of IEEE 802.1 standards for deterministic communication over standard Ethernet networks.

**Trajectory Planning**: The process of creating a path that a robot will follow, including timing information about how fast it should move.

**Transformer Architecture**: A deep learning model that uses attention mechanisms to weigh the importance of input data, widely used in natural language processing.

## U

**Unified Robot Description Format (URDF)**: An XML format for representing robot models including links, joints, and other elements.

**Universal Robots (UR)**: A category of collaborative robots designed to work safely alongside humans.

**Unstructured Environment**: An environment without a fixed or predetermined layout, requiring robots to adapt their behavior dynamically.

## V

**Variational Autoencoder (VAE)**: A type of artificial neural network used to learn efficient codings of unlabeled data.

**Visual Servoing**: A control technique that uses visual feedback from a camera to control the motion of a robot.

## W

**Whole-Body Control**: A control strategy that coordinates all the degrees of freedom of a robot simultaneously to achieve multiple tasks.

**Wheeled Inverted Pendulum (WIP)**: A dynamic model for mobile robots that balances on wheels like a pendulum.

## X

**X-bar Representation**: A method for representing spatial relationships in robotics and computer vision, often used in state estimation.

## Y

**Yaw**: The rotation of a robot around its vertical axis, changing its heading direction.

## Z

**Zero-Moment Point (ZMP)**: A concept in bipedal locomotion for dynamic balance, representing a point where the net moment due to gravity and inertia forces equals zero.

**Z-axis**: The vertical axis in a three-dimensional coordinate system, typically representing height or elevation.
